Less than 60% of adults with advanced chronic kidney disease in a healthcare system see a kidney doctor

July 2019

Timely referral to specialist kidney care services for people with chronic kidney disease (CKD) has potential benefits in slowing CKD progression and preventing other complications. Clinical practice guidelines recommend that people with estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) < 30 ml/min/1.73m2 (advanced CKD) receive care from a nephrologist or kidney doctor.1 In 2018, in the Veterans Affairs (VA) Health System, the percentage of adults having seen a nephrologist during the fiscal year varied by age group from 32%–59% among people with eGFR 15–29 and from 28%–50% among people with eGFR <15 .* The lower percentages were among people aged 70 years or older. This suggests a potential opportunity to improve referral to nephrology care in US veteran adults with advanced CKD, and in particular among those aged 70 years or older.

*The eGFR is a measure of kidney function; the lower the eGFR, the more advanced the disease (e.g., eGFR <15 ml/min/1.73m2 is kidney failure). Veterans aged 65 and older frequently use Medicare coverage in combination with the VA; care provided through Medicare is not included in this indicator. Additionally, nephrology visits are only those to an outpatient nephrology clinic and not to a dialysis or transplant facility. Patients with eGFR <30 ml/min/1.73m2 (advanced CKD) may be seen directly at one of these clinics.
