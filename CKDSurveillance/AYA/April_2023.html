Awareness of Kidney Disease in the U.S. is Increasing, but Remains Low

April 2023

According to CDC, more than 1 in 7, an estimated 37 million US adults, have chronic kidney disease (CKD), and as many as 9 in 10 adults with CKD do not know they have it.1 This lack of awareness is likely driven by the fact that kidney disease is asymptomatic for much of its clinical course, and detection and diagnosis require testing both blood and urine. Awareness of CKD at the population, patient, health systems, and policymaking levels can maximize prevention efforts. Detection in earlier stages of CKD, as well as clear patient-physician communication, can ensure effective treatment for those who most need it.

Data on adults aged ≥ 18 years from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES, 2001–March 2020) reveal that the overall awareness of CKD stages 3–4 was low (11.2%) among adults who have laboratory evidence of the disease. Awareness began to rise in 2009 and ultimately reached 20% overall in the most recent NHANES cycle (2017–March 2020 pre-pandemic cycle). Awareness is higher among individuals with more advanced CKD, but the trend toward increased awareness is seen in all stages. The most significant change is seen in adults with stage 4 kidney disease (eGFR 15-29 ml/min/1.73m2), where awareness increased from 35.7% in the 2001–2004 cycle to 62.6% in the 2017–March 2020 pre-pandemic cycle.

While this improvement is encouraging, there are opportunities to continue raising awareness of CKD, not only in patients with the condition, but also in health care providers. For instance, opportunities exist for earlier screening and diagnosis, as well as helping patients to make appropriate lifestyle changes to lose weight if needed and manage blood pressure and glycemic levels. Patients can also be guided in appropriate use of prescription drugs, including angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, angiotensin receptor blockers, and sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors. Coordinating the work of researchers, medical practitioners, health care administrators, and policy makers may support efforts to raise awareness and prevent CKD and its progression.
