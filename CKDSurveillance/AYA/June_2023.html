Revealing Geographic Patterns: Mapping Poverty and Chronic Kidney Disease in the United States

June 2023

Poverty is a major social determinant of health that is closely linked to chronic kidney disease (CKD). People who live in low-income neighborhoods face challenges that may contribute to the development and progression of CKD, such as poor education, food insecurity, or exposure to environmental toxins.1,2 People with lower incomes also often have less access to preventive health care services, timely diagnosis, and optimal management of CKD.1,2

Based on data from the American Community Survey (2015–2019) and the Medicare 5% random sample (2019), geographic variations exist in both poverty level and prevalence of diagnosed CKD. The map below shows county-level distributions of poverty and diagnosed CKD and allows for simultaneous comparison of the two measurements and identification of areas where high levels of both poverty and diagnosed CKD coexist. For example, the dark-blue counties (as are found in much of Alabama, Georgia, and South Carolina) show high levels of both poverty and diagnosed CKD; plum-colored counties in Florida, Pennsylvania, and North Dakota show low levels of poverty and high levels of diagnosed CKD.

These results suggest the need to examine geographic data in future CKD studies in terms of resource allocation, health disparities, quality of care, and health policy.
