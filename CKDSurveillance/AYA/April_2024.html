Lack of Health Insurance Is Common Among US Adults with Chronic Kidney Disease

April 2024

Adults with no health insurance are more likely to delay or forgo care because of costs, including preventive care and services for acute and chronic illnesses.1 The 2010 Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act (ACA) reduced the number of uninsured individuals through health insurance exchanges, the dependent coverage provision, Medicaid expansion, availability of subsidies, and other policy changes.2,3

We assessed the percentage of US adults aged 18 to 64 years who lacked health insurance coverage, by chronic kidney disease (CKD) status, using data from the US National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey 2005−March 2020. CKD was defined by the presence of albuminuria (urine albumin to creatinine ratio – UACR ≥ 30 mg/g) or estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) < 60 ml/min/1.73m2. Both crude and age-standardized estimates are presented, using the 2010 US Census population as the standard population.

Overall, the crude estimates show that around 22% of US adults aged 18−64 years lacked health insurance coverage in 2005−2008. After a peak during 2009−2012, the estimates have declined to 17.9% for adults with CKD and 16.1% for adults without CKD in 2017−2020. After age standardization, prevalence of lack of health insurance coverage declined faster among adults with than without CKD.

Since the enactment of the ACA from 2010 and its progressive implementation in subsequent years, there has been a decline in the percentage of U.S. adults without health insurance. Yet, adults with CKD who remain without health care coverage are at higher risk of not receiving the timely screening, follow-up and treatment required to prevent progression of the disease and other associated complications. Efforts to ensure availability of adequate health insurance are important for earlier detection and optimal management of CKD.
