Albuminuria Testing Among U.S. Veterans on the Rise, but Still Low

March 2025

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) typically remains without symptoms until it progresses to advanced stages. Urine albumin-creatinine ratio (uACR) is a test used to detect increased albuminuria, a marker of kidney damage and the earliest indicator of CKD in diabetes and hypertension. In clinical practice, both uACR and blood testing for serum creatinine to estimate glomerular filtration rate (eGFR, a measure of kidney function) are used to screen, diagnose, and manage CKD.1,2 Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) clinical practice guidelines recommend at least annual testing for individuals with diabetes mellitus and those with established CKD.3,4

National data from the Veterans Health Administration System (2006 to 2022) show that albuminuria testing rates for veterans increased from 7.2% to 15.4%.5 Albuminuria testing for veterans with diabetes rose from 21.9% to 40.2% and for those with hypertension from 9.7% to 22.5% (Figure).

Although albuminuria testing has gradually increased over time among veterans, less than half of those with diabetes received the recommended screening in 2022 and less than a quarter of those with hypertension were tested.5 Screening for CKD is important for early detection and treatment to prevent progression to kidney failure and other complications among those with diabetes or hypertension, or with other risk factors for kidney disease such as older age, family history of kidney disease or hypertension, and cardiovascular disease.
