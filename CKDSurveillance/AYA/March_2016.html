Chronic Kidney Disease and Smoking

Smoking is a significant modifiable risk factor associated with chronic kidney disease (CKD), as well as a risk factor for poor health and early death in the general population. Also, women who smoke during pregnancy risk having a child with kidney damage.

As shown by the graph, during 1999-2012, the estimated prevalence of current smoking among those with CKD decreased as their disease became more severe: 31% among people in the earliest stage of CKD (stage 1), 22% among those with stage 2 CKD, and 11%-12% among people with advanced stages of CKD (stages 3-4). The prevalence of current smoking in the general population was estimated at 23%.

Although individuals in advanced stages of CKD were less likely to be current smokers than those in earlier stages or the general population, they were more likely to have been past smokers. One possible explanation for this trend is that as individuals with CKD become sicker, they are more likely to stop smoking.


Footnote:

The prevalence is age-adjusted.

Stage Definitions:

Stage 1: Kidney damage; Stage 2: Mildly reduced kidney function; Stage 3: Moderately reduced kidney function; Stage 4: Severely reduced kidney function; Stage 5: End-stage kidney failure
