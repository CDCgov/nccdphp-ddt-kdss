Rising Household Food Insecurity Among Adults With Chronic Kidney Disease in the United States

October 2023

Food insecurity is a socioeconomic situation at the household level where there is limited or uncertain access to adequate food for a healthy lifestyle. Food insecurity is associated with an increased risk of many chronic diseases, such as diabetes, obesity, heart disease, mental health disorders, and chronic kidney disease.1

As part of CDC’s Kidney Disease Surveillance System2, we analyzed US data on food insecurity among adults aged 18 years or older, from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (2001–March 2020). Household food insecurity was estimated by using the US Food Security Survey Module.3 Adults were considered to be food insecure if they reported living in a household with marginal, low, or very low food security. During this period, the crude prevalence of food insecurity changed similarly among those with or without chronic kidney disease (CKD), having nearly doubled by 2017–March 2020 (from 16.5% in 2001–2004 for those with CKD and those without, to 31.0% and 27.8% in 2017—March 2020, respectively, P < .001). After the 2001–2004 cycle, age-standardized prevalence of food insecurity was significantly higher among adults with CKD than among those without CKD.

Food insecurity has increased over the past 2 decades in the United States and remains higher among those adults with CKD than among those without. Adults with CKD who report being food insecure are at a higher risk of disease progression than those who are food secure.1 Strategies to identify households and individuals at risk of food insecurity, as well as those who are persistently food insecure, could guide interventions to improve their health status, disease management, and quality of life.
