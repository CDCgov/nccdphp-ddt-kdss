Racial/Ethnic Differences in Nephrologist Visits among US Veterans with Advanced Kidney Disease

February 2024

As kidney disease progresses, patients require referral to a nephrologist for co-managed care, establishment of a diagnosis, slowing progression, and/or managing complications of the disease. Referral to a nephrologist is recommended when the estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) drops below 30 ml/min/1.73 m2, i.e., stage 4 chronic kidney disease (CKD).1

Using data from the national Veterans Health Administration (VHA) from 2018 to 2022, we show the percent of US veterans with stage 4 CKD who had at least one nephrology visit per year. During the five years, between 42% and 51% of non-Hispanic Black veterans were seeing a nephrologist. Fewer non-Hispanic White veterans had nephrologist visits, ranging from 32% to 36%. No definite trend was seen over time, although a drop was observed between 2020 and 2021, possibly due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Overall, less than half of US veterans with stage 4 CKD attended VHA Nephrology Clinics in any given year. While this finding may represent a gap in guideline-directed referral practices to nephrologists, it may also be the result of nephrology care being provided outside the VHA to US veterans. Black US veterans with stage 4 CKD showed the highest representation among those seeing a nephrologist in the VHA, where all racial/ethnic groups have an equal opportunity to access health care. These findings may reflect disparities in advanced kidney disease, with higher burden of advanced kidney disease among the Black population, which may be deserving of greater attention by health systems, policy makers, and the public health system.2, 3
