Cardiovascular Disease Is Common in Older US Adults and Is the Leading Cause of Death in People With Chronic Kidney Disease

September 2019

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) and chronic kidney disease (CKD) often occur together and share many of the same risk factors, including high blood pressure, diabetes, and older age.1 People with CKD are at risk of developing CVD, and CVD, in turn, is the leading cause of death among people with CKD. In 2017, over 860,000 people died—about 1 in 3 deaths— from CVD in the United States.1 Data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) showed that in 2015–2016, compared with younger adults, CVD* was more common in adults aged 70 years or older (26%) and in adults aged 60 to 69 years (15%). Given the high percentage of CVD among adults aged 60 years or older, public health efforts to raise awareness of both CVD and CKD and their common risk factors may help reduce this leading cause of death among adults with CKD.

1Benjamin EJ, Muntner P, Alonso A, et al. Heart disease and stroke statistics—2019 update: a report from the American Heart Association. Circulation. 2019;139(10):e233-e248. doi: 10.1161/CIR.0000000000000659.
*CVD was defined as self-report of coronary heart disease, angina, or previous myocardial infarction or stroke.
