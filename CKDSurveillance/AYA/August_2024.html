Daily Dietary Sodium and Potassium Intake Among Adults With Chronic Kidney Disease

August 2024

Sodium and potassium are electrolytes with important functions in the body, such as maintenance of blood pressure and neuromuscular function. The current federal recommendations are a daily intake of less than 2,300 mg of sodium for adults and at least 3,400 mg of potassium for men and 2,600 mg of potassium for women.1,2,3 Among adults with chronic kidney disease (CKD), excessive intake of sodium or inadequate potassium intake may worsen health outcomes.4,5

Data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey show that most adults do not consume the recommended levels of sodium and potassium. A higher percentage of adults with CKD consumed the recommended amount of sodium compared to adults without CKD. However, the percentage of adults with CKD consuming the recommended amount declined from 2005–2008 (38.4% ) to March 2017 (32.8%). Fewer adults with CKD met the recommended potassium intake (29%) than adults without CKD (35%), without significant changes over time.

A balanced diet with appropriate intake of sodium and potassium is necessary for maintaining optimal health, reducing the risk of new CKD, or slowing its progression. Referring patients to nutritional counseling as part of medical care may optimize dietary intake and play an important role in the management of blood pressure, maintenance of a healthy weight, and correction of electrolyte abnormalities such as hyperkalemia or hypokalemia.6 See CDC’s effects of sodium and potassium for more information.2
