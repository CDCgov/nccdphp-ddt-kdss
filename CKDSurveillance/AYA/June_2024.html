Awareness of Chronic Kidney Disease Remains Low Among U.S. Adults

June 2024

Over 35 million adults in the United States have chronic kidney disease (CKD), and 9 in 10 adults with CKD do not know they have it.1 While CKD awareness has improved during the last two decades, it remains low, with recent data indicating that only about 25% adults with moderate or advanced CKD (G3-G5) are aware of having the disease.2

Data on U.S. adults aged 20 years and older participating in the 2017–March 2020 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) indicate that awareness of CKD was low among adults with moderately increased risk for CKD (yellow cells in the table), who are also at low risk for long-term complications. Prevalence of awareness generally increased with advancing CKD and was highest among adults with severely decreased kidney function (G4) and moderately or severely increased albuminuria.

Although earlier CKD categories (G1-G3a) are more prevalent than more advanced categories (G3b-G5), those with moderately increased risk for CKD (yellow cells in the table), representing 22 million U.S. adults, are often not aware of their diagnosis. Most of these adults are asymptomatic or may never have been screened for CKD. Additionally, 1 million U.S. adults with CKD G3b-G4 are not yet aware of their diagnosis. The gap in CKD awareness, particularly among those with moderately increased risk for CKD, could be addressed by screening and education for CKD among those with risk factors, such as diabetes mellitus or hypertension. This step has the potential to raise awareness and initiate secondary prevention efforts much earlier in CKD to slow progression and reduce associated complications.
