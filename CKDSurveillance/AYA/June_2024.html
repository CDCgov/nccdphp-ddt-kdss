Awareness of Chronic Kidney Disease remains low among US Adults

June 2024

Over 35 million adults in the United States have chronic kidney disease (CKD), and 9 in 10 adults with CKD do not know they have it.1 While CKD awareness has improved during the last two decades it remains low with only about 25% adults with moderate or advanced CKD (G3-G5) being aware of having the disease based on more recent data.2

Data on US adults aged 20 years and older participating in the 2017–March 2020 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) show that awareness of CKD was lowest among adults with early stages of CKD (yellow cells in the Table), who are also at low risk for long-term complications. Prevalence of awareness increased with advancing CKD and was highest among people with severely decreased kidney function (G4).

While earlier CKD categories (G1-G3a) are more prevalent than more advanced categories (G3b-G5), adults in earlier categories of CKD are less likely to be aware of their CKD, as most of these individuals are usually asymptomatic and or may never have been screened for the condition. The gap of CKD awareness, particularly in the earlier categories of CKD affecting millions of US adults could be addressed by screening for CKD among those with risk factors such as diabetes mellitus or hypertension. This step has the potential toward raising awareness and initiating secondary prevention efforts much earlier in CKD to slow progression and reduce associated complications.

Legend: *Green=Low risk; Yellow=Moderately increased risk; Orange=High risk; Red=Very high risk

Awareness of CKD was defined by self-reported affirmative response to the question “Have you ever been told by a doctor or other health professional that you had weak or failing kidneys?” among participants with CKD. CKD was defined by a urine albumin to creatinine ratio (ACR) ≥30 mg/g or estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) ≤60 ml/min/1.73m2.3
