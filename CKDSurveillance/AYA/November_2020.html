Awareness of Chronic Kidney Disease in US Adults With Diabetes Remains Low

November 2020

Diabetes is a leading cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) and a major risk factor in the progression to kidney failure or end-stage kidney disease. An estimated 34.1 million US adults have diabetes,1 and prevalence is projected to increase globally in the coming decade.2 Prevalence of CKD in adults with diabetes has also increased;3 however, awareness remains low. Data from the 2011-2014 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey show that among US adults with diabetes and advanced CKD (stages 3 and 4),* 21.3% were aware of having CKD, similar to previous survey years (19.2% in 2005-2010 and 22.6% in 1999-2004). People with diabetes have a higher risk of premature death and this risk increases in the presence of CKD.4 Enhanced and innovative efforts in primary and specialty care to increase testing and awareness of CKD in adults with diabetes are crucial to help guide patient care and management and improve the health of people with diabetes.

* Stage 3 is defined as an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) of 30-59 ml/min/1.73 m², and Stage 4 is defined as an eGFR of 15-29 ml/min/1.73 m².
