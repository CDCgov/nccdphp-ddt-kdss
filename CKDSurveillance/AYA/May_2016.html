High Blood Pressure in the US population with Chronic Kidney Disease

High blood pressure (hypertension) is, after diabetes, the second leading cause of chronic kidney disease (CKD) in the United States. In people with CKD, high blood pressure is associated with a higher risk of heart diseases and stroke. As shown in the figure, for the period 2011 to 2012, the prevalence of high blood pressure in the CKD population was 61% and has not changed appreciably since 1999.

Controlling high blood pressure, as well as high blood sugar and cholesterol all risk factors for heart disease and stroke is very important. People with CKD can improve their outcomes by controlling these risk factors .

Footnote:

Estimates are adjusted for age.

Hypertension was assessed by self-report, a blood pressure measurement ≥140/≥90 mmHg, or hypertension drug use. CKD was diagnosed by laboratory testing and defined by an estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) a measure of kidney function of 15-59 ml/min/1.73 m or an eGFR ≥60 ml/min/1.73 m in the presence of albuminuria (that is, protein in the urine). eGFR <15 ml/min/1.73 m was excluded.
