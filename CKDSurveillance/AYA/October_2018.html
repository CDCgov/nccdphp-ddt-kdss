Diagnosed Diabetes Decreased in Adults Aged 60–64 years in a Commercially Insured Population

October 2018

Diabetes is a major risk factor for chronic kidney disease (CKD). Data from Optum Clinformatic showed the percentage of commercially insured patients with diagnosed diabetes* remained constant from 2006 to 2016 for people aged 20–39 years, and increased from 2006 until 2011 and then leveled off through 2016 for people aged 40–59 years. In contrast, for those aged 60–64 years, the percentage with diagnosed diabetes increased from 10.6% in 2006 to 13.3% in 2011 and then declined to 12.5% by 2016. This downward trend in people aged 60–64 years may be due to greater awareness of, and improvements in, management of type 2 diabetes risk factors likely to impact incidence of diabetes-related complications, including CKD. Efforts may be considered to sustain and improve this trend in people aged 60–64 years and to bring about a decline in younger ages.

ICD-CM: International Classification of Diseases—Clinical Modification.
*Diabetes defined through the use of ICD-CM diagnosis codes (9th and 10th revisions) in claims from Optum Clinformatic, which provides paid medical and prescription claims and enrollment information for participants in the commercial insurance plans and Medicare Advantage plans of a large US managed-care health insurance company.
