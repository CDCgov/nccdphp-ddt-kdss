Prevalence of Nocturia Among U.S. Adults With and Without Chronic Kidney Disease

January 2025

Nocturnal (nighttime) urinary frequency, or nocturia, is defined as waking up from sleep to urinate more than once per night. Causes include lower urinary obstruction (e.g., due to prostate enlargement in men); late-night meals; excessive fluid, alcohol, or caffeine intake close to bedtime; excessive urine output seen in diabetes mellitus; diuretic medications; kidney disease; heart failure; or sleep apnea. People with nocturia may report daytime fatigue due to disruption of their sleep.1, 2, 3, 4 Nocturia is one of the most common urinary symptoms, occurring more often as people age and affecting over half of adults age 50 or older.

Between 2005 and 2020, crude prevalence of nocturia was higher in U.S. adults with chronic kidney disease (CKD) than those without CKD (in 2017–March 2020, 40.2% among those with CKD stage 1 or 2 and 50.2% among those with CKD stage 3–5 vs. 25.7% among those with no CKD). Throughout the study period, crude prevalence of nocturia was higher in more advanced CKD stages (3–5). Age-standardized prevalence, however, was similar among all CKD stages but higher than in those without CKD, except in the 2013–2016 cohort. Age-standardized prevalence of nocturia among adults with any CKD ranged from 31.7% in 2005–2008 to 39.4% in 2017–March 2020, compared to a range of 22.6%–26.0% among those without CKD for the same years.

While nocturia was more common among U.S. adults with CKD than those without CKD, it remained relatively stable over time. Some underlying causes may be easily correctable, such as avoidance of late-night meals and fluids/alcohol/caffeinated drinks too close to bedtime. However, nocturia can also be a sign of a more serious underlying condition such as urinary obstruction or CKD.
