Reported Causes of Kidney Failure in Children, Adolescents, and Adults

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a condition in which kidneys are damaged over time and cannot filter blood as well as healthy kidneys. Because of this, wastes from the blood remain in the body and may cause other health problems, including kidney failure. As seen in the chart, reported causes of kidney failure or end-stage renal disease (ESRD) in children and adolescents in the United States differ greatly from causes of ESRD reported in adults.

For the years 2011-2013, the leading causes of ESRD in children and adolescents aged 21 years or younger were glomerulonephritis and other causes. Glomerulonephritis is an inflammation of the kidneys that can be caused by other diseases or is of unknown cause. Other causes included tumors, complications of transplanted organs, interstitial nephritis (a kidney disorder in which the spaces between the kidney tubules become swollen), and congenital disease.

The percentage of children and adolescents with ESRD who had a diagnosis of glomerulonephritis increased with age (from 9% among those aged 0-4 years to 38% among those aged 18-21 years). In contrast, the percentage of children and adolescents with ESRD who had a diagnosis of other causes decreased with age (from 71% among those aged 0-4 years to 42% among those aged 18-21 years). For adults aged 22-44 years during the same 2011-2013 period, glomerulonephritis (33%), diabetes (21%), and hypertension (21%) were the leading causes of ESRD. These three conditions increase the risk of developing kidney failure at any age. Therefore, prevention and proper management of these conditions might offer the greatest benefit for preventing or delaying CKD and kidney failure.


Note: For the purposes of this analysis, individuals aged 21 years or younger are considered children and adolescents, while individuals aged 22-44 years are considered adults.

Source:

Saran R, Li Y, Robinson B, et al. US Renal Data System 2015 Annual Data Report: epidemiology of kidney disease in the United States .    Am J Kidney Dis . 2016;67(3)(suppl 1):S1-S434.
