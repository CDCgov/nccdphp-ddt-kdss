Prevalence of Cardiovascular-Kidney-Metabolic Syndrome Is Rising Among U.S. Adults

August 2025

Cardiovascular-kidney-metabolic (CKM) syndrome is defined as a health condition resulting from the interactions between obesity, diabetes mellitus, chronic kidney disease (CKD), and cardiovascular diseases (CVD).1 The presence of CKM syndrome increases the risk for end-organ damage (including CKD) and premature death. National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey data show prevalence of CKM syndrome (stages 1–4) among adults 20 years and older is high and has increased from 84.6% in 2001–2004 to 89.2% in 2017–March 2020. A 1% to 2% increase in prevalence was observed for each stage, 1–4, between 2001 and March 2020, with a corresponding decline in stage 0 (15.4% to 10.8%). The largest increase in CKM syndrome prevalence (from 72.6% to 78.7%) was observed among younger ages (20–39 years). CKM syndrome prevalence was higher for males than females but increased more for females. Prevalence was highest among non-Hispanic Black adults but increased for all race and ethnicity groups, especially among Hispanic and non-Hispanic White adults.

This rising prevalence of early-stage CKM syndrome (stages 1–2) indicates opportunities for earlier detection and management of obesity and screening for components of CKM syndrome (including CKD). These efforts could prevent progression to clinical stages of CKM syndrome (stages 3–4), including advanced CKD (very high-risk CKD as per the Kidney Disease Improving Global Outcomes [KDIGO] guideline2) and CVD.
