High Burden of Sleep Problems Among People with Chronic Kidney Disease

In the 2011-2014 National Health Examination and Nutrition Survey (NHANES),* about 15%-16% (about 31-34 million) of the U.S. population aged 20 years or older had chronic kidney disease (CKD) stages 1-4 (not on dialysis).1 A high proportion of both people with CKD and people on chronic dialysis have been reported to have trouble sleeping and inadequate sleep.2-4

Based on estimates from the 2005-2010 NHANES, the percentage reporting "trouble sleeping" was higher among those with CKD (about 30%) than among those without CKD (24%). The percentage of adults reporting "sleep disorder" was also higher among those with CKD (about 10% compared with 7% for those without CKD). The percentage reporting nocturia (needing to wake 2 or more times during the night to use the bathroom) was higher among people in the more advanced stages of CKD than among the other groups (45% among those in CKD stages 3-4 versus 36% among those in CKD stages 1-2 and 21% among those without CKD). In contrast, the percentage reporting inadequate sleep was lower among people in the more advanced stages of CKD than among the other groups (31% among those in CKD stages 3-4 versus 42% among those in CKD stages 1-2 and 37% among those without CKD).

Self-reported poor sleep quality has been associated with lower quality of life and premature death.2-4 These data highlight the importance of better managing sleep problems in people with CKD and determining whether better management of sleep problems can improve outcomes among those with CKD.

 

Footnote:

People with kidney damage (those having albumin in the urine) or with an estimated glomerular filtration rate-a measure of kidney function-less than 60 mL/min per 1.73 m2 for 3 months or longer are considered to have CKD. CKD has various levels or stages of seriousness. The higher the stage, the more advanced the disease. Inadequate sleep was defined as reported sleep of less than 6 hours per night on average. Trouble sleeping and doctor's recognition of sleep disorders were self-reported.

* Prevalence estimates were obtained from NHANES using the Modification of Diet in Renal Disease (MDRD) Study equation.
