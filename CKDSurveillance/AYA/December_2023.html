Self-Reported Physical Activity among US Adults with and without Chronic Kidney Disease

December 2023

Regular physical activity (PA) can improve many aspects of health - weight management, strength, balance, cardio-metabolic fitness, and well-being1. However, only one in four Americans aged 18 years or older met the 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines for both aerobic (e.g., running/walking) and muscle strengthening activities in 2020.2 Increasing the level of PA may provide a range of benefits in patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD), including slower decline in kidney function, and improve physical function, cardiovascular disease risk, and quality of life.3, 4

Among adults aged 18 years or older participating in the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES, 2001–March 2020), prevalence of crude and age-standardized moderate or vigorous PA increased significantly over time, among both participants with and without CKD (Figure)5. The nearly 13% increase in prevalence of PA among those with CKD represents 7 million adults. However, the prevalence of PA was consistently lower among adults with CKD than those without CKD. Being physically active was defined by responses to two questions on two categories of activities in the PA questionnaire in NHANES – (i) fitness, sports or recreation-related or (ii) work-related activities during a routine week – based on whether such activities involved large (vigorous activity) or small (moderate activity) increase in heart rate for at least 10 minutes.

The observed trends in rising PA among those with CKD are somewhat reassuring given many barriers to being active among CKD patients due to the disease and its associated comorbidities. In addition, CKD patients living in high-poverty neighborhoods have higher risk of CKD progression, and people who live in high-poverty neighborhoods may have reduced access to resources that support physical activity, such as green space6 and walkable neighborhoods. There may be a need for large, well-conducted randomized clinical trials to inform and motivate systematic implementation of PA programs in clinical practice, for individuals living with different stages of CKD across the lifespan.
