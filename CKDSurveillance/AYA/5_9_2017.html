Long-term Trends in Prevalence of Chronic Kidney Disease Among People With and Without High Blood Pressure in the United States

High blood pressure, or hypertension (HTN), is one of the leading risk factors for chronic kidney disease (CKD) in the United States. We examined trends in CKD prevalence in various HTN categories (i.e., diagnosed, undiagnosed, pre-, and no HTN) over time.* Among a nationally representative sample of US adults, the prevalence of CKD among adults with diagnosed HTN remained relatively stable from 1999-2004 (36.5%) to 2011-2014 (35.8%), and the prevalence of CKD among adults with undiagnosed HTN decreased significantly, from 26.9% to 20.4% over the same period. In addition, the prevalence of CKD in the pre-HTN and no HTN groups continued to be fairly similar from the period 1999-2004 to 2011-2014. The large magnitude of the CKD prevalence rates in the diagnosed and undiagnosed HTN groups supports the importance of national public health efforts that aim to improve blood pressure control and delay new cases of CKD.

Footnote: Estimates are unadjusted.

* Diagnosed hypertension (HTN) was defined as a self-reported diagnosis (told by a medical provider that they had high blood pressure [BP]); undiagnosed HTN, systolic BP (SBP) ≥140 mm Hg or diastolic BP (DBP) ≥90 mm Hg, without a self-reported diagnosis; and pre-HTN, 120≤SBP<140 mm Hg or 80≤DBP<90 mm Hg. CKD was defined as estimated glomerular filtration rate of <60 mL/min/1.73 m2 (CKD-EPI equation) or albumin-to-creatinine ratio ≥30 mg/g.
