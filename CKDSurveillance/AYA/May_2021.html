More Than Half of US Veterans with Chronic Kidney Disease Being Treated for High Blood Pressure Are Not Meeting Blood Pressure Targets

May 2021

High blood pressure (BP), or hypertension, is a leading cause of kidney failure in the United States.1 In people with chronic kidney disease (CKD), optimal BP control is critical to slow down progression to kidney failure.2

From 2006 to 2018, about 94% of 12 million US veterans with CKD and high BP had a health provider-documented diagnosis code (ICD-9 or ICD-10) of hypertension (Figure). During this period, the percentage of veterans with diagnosed hypertension who were on BP-lowering medications but did not have their BP under control (BP > 130/80 mmHg) declined from 57.6% to 51.3%. The percentage who had their BP under control (BP ≤ 130/80 mmHg) increased from 30.2% in 2006 to 32.5% in 2010 but declined to 25.2% in 2018. On the other hand, the percentage with diagnosed hypertension who were not receiving BP-lowering medications rose from 5.9% to 17.4%. The percentage of veterans with CKD and high BP who did not have a diagnosis of hypertension remained between 5% and 6% throughout the period.

These results suggest a high degree of provider awareness of hypertension in the setting of an integrated health care system, as indicated by patients’ recorded diagnosis of hypertension. However, despite this provider awareness, more than 50% of patients with diagnosed hypertension in 2018 were not achieving the guideline-based BP target of 130/80 mmHg. Designing and implementing quality improvement programs will help improve this practice gap.
