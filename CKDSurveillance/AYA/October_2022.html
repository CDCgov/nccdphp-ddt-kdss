Chronic Kidney Disease Is Prevalent Among Adults with Prediabetes or Undiagnosed Diabetes

October 2022

In the United States, 96 million individuals (38% of adults) are affected by prediabetes, which is when blood sugar levels are higher than normal, but not yet high enough to be diagnosed as type 2 diabetes.1 Although prediabetes is potentially reversible, it can progress to type 2 diabetes, which affects all major organs in the body, including the kidneys, blood vessels, nerves, and eyes. It is not widely known that prediabetes is also an important risk factor for chronic kidney disease (CKD), in addition to diabetes, hypertension, and obesity.2,3

Data on adults aged 18 years or older from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES, 2001-2020) reveal that the prevalence of CKD stages 3–4, when kidney function is less than 60 ml/min/1.73 m², is similar among individuals with prediabetes and those with undiagnosed diabetes. During this period, the prevalence of CKD stages 3–4 was approximately 4% (~5 million adults) among individuals without diabetes, 10%–14% among individuals with prediabetes (~5 million adults) or undiagnosed diabetes (~0.6 million adults), and approximately 20% among individuals with diagnosed diabetes (~5 million adults).

Healthy habits, such as regular physical activity and healthy diet, can improve risk factors for diabetes. Healthy dietary habits include increasing consumption of plant-based foods, reducing consumption of red meat, lowering intake of refined sugar and carbohydrates, and limiting sodium intake. Evidence-based programs have enormous potential to prevent diabetes and save millions of lives and dollars.4 Future studies could investigate the value of screening for the presence of kidney disease among those with prediabetes to prevent downstream consequences of progressive kidney disease.
