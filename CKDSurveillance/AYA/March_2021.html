Adults With Early-Stage Chronic Kidney Disease Are More Likely To Currently Smoke

March 2021

In 2019, an estimated 34 million US adults reported current cigarette smoking1. Smoking causes cancer, lung disease, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease2. Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death among people with chronic kidney disease (CKD); many are likely to die of heart disease or stroke before reaching kidney failure, or end-stage renal disease3,4. In addition, smoking may be associated with a higher risk of CKD progression5.

However, smoking is a risk factor that can be changed. Data from the 2011–2016 National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey show that the percentage of adults with CKD who formerly smoked was similar across CKD stages and compared with the general population†. The percentage of adults with CKD who currently smoke decreased with increasing severity of CKD: prevalence of current smoking was higher in adults with CKD stage 1 (27.8%) than in those with CKD stage 3 (13.7%) or those in the general population (19.5%). The declining percentage of current smoking with increasing CKD severity may in part reflect smoking cessation as CKD worsens.

Smoking cessation in the early stages of CKD may not only reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease but also slow CKD progression6,7. The high rates of current smoking in the early stages of CKD emphasize an opportunity for primary care and specialty care providers to educate patients about these health risks along with providing treatment and resources to assist with smoking cessation.

†Stages of CKD are defined as: stage 1, estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) —a measure of kidney function— ≥ 90 ml/min/1.73 m2 and presence of albuminuria (protein in the urine, a marker of kidney damage); stage 2, eGFR 60-89 ml/min/1.73 m2 and presence of albuminuria; stage 3, eGFR 30-59 ml/min/1.73 m2; stage 4, eGFR 15-29 ml/min/1.73 m2. Stage 5, eGFR < 15 ml/min/1.73 m2 (kidney failure), was excluded. The general population includes people with and without CKD.
